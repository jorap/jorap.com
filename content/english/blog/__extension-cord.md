Nobody writes love letters to extension cords. They're the duct tape of electricity - unglamorous, always missing when you need one, and quietly dangerous when you buy the wrong one.

I learned that last part the hard way after a cheap cord got warm under a load it had no business carrying. Since then I've treated extension cords like tools, not afterthoughts.

---

## Length: buy longer than you think (but not ridiculous)

The cord that's **six feet too short** is useless. The cord that's **thirty feet too long** becomes a trip hazard and a tangled mess behind the desk.

I measure the run once - wall outlet to desk, TV to nearest plug, outdoor path to the grill - and add a little slack. For home office use I like **10–15 feet**. For travel, a compact **6-foot** cord with a flat plug wins.

---

## Gauge matters when watts matter

Thin cords are fine for lamps and phone chargers. They are **not fine** for space heaters, high-wattage tools, or a desk full of gear drawing serious power.

Look for **14 AWG for heavier loads**, 16 AWG for moderate household use. If the package doesn't say, that's a no from me.

When a cord feels warm under normal use, something is wrong - undersized cord, damaged insulation, or too many things daisy-chained. Unplug and fix it.

---

## Flat plugs and right-angle heads save real space

Behind a bookshelf or TV stand, a **flat plug** that sits flush against the wall beats a chunky one that pushes furniture out an inch. Right-angle heads help when the outlet is behind something immovable.

Small detail. Huge difference in tight spaces.

---

## Surge protection vs. plain extension

A **surge protector** is an extension cord with insurance. For computers, monitors, and anything you can't afford to lose to a spike, I use a rated surge strip - not a dollar-store splitter.

Plain extension cords are fine for lamps, holiday lights, and temporary runs where surge isn't the point.

---

## Outdoor cords are a different species

Outdoor means **weather-rated jacket**, grounded plug, and storing it dry when you're done. Indoor cords outside are how people learn vocabulary they didn't want to learn.

---

## Safety rules I actually follow

- **No daisy chains** of surge strips and extensions - pick one proper run
- **Uncoil long cords** if they're carrying serious load; bundled heat builds up
- **Replace** anything with cracked insulation, bent prongs, or mystery warmth
- **Don't walk on cords** as a permanent plan - foot traffic kills jacketing

---

## My two-cord system

**Home office:** one quality surge strip at the desk, one 12-foot extension with a flat plug for the awkward wall behind the shelf.

**Travel / events:** one light 6-foot cord in the laptop bag. Hotel rooms always put the outlet behind the bed across the room. Always.

That second one also shows up on my **remote work checklist** - because a dead battery at the wrong outlet is a meeting killer.

---

## Bottom line

Extension cords are not interchangeable commodities. Length, gauge, plug shape, and whether you need surge protection all depend on the job.

Buy once for the job, label them if you're the household IT person, and retire anything that smells like warm plastic.
