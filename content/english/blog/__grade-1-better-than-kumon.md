I gave my son a choice: **Kumon math or Kumon reading.**

He picked reading. No drama. He already **reads well** - chapter books at six, not struggling through each line. Kumon math looked like a lot of sitting still with a pencil. The reading track won.

**We went with reading on purpose anyway.** Not to teach him to read. Kumon calls it "reading," but what we wanted was **handwriting** - pencil grip, copying, spelling, getting words onto paper without a meltdown. Not story writing. The physical part. He'll decode anything you hand him. Ask him to handwrite three sentences and the pencil goes limp.

That's the gap. Kumon reading is mostly worksheets and daily copy reps. Boring if you don't need the reading ladder. Perfect if you need the **handwriting** ladder and want someone else to run it.

He's six, grade 1, and he's on the reading track now. Kumon runs the **rep layer** - copy work, spelling drills, someone else saying do it again tomorrow. I'm not trying to replace that.

What I'm building at home is **handwriting he'll actually use** and **math games that are better than Kumon would be**. Kumon will chase neater letters through rows. Home has to make him pick up the pencil for something he cares about. Math disguised as **games** - because he told me straight up: he doesn't like math. **It's harder.**

Reading clicks for him. Math doesn't yet. He'll blast through five quiz cards if it's a race. He'll bolt if I slide a worksheet across the table. He doesn't want more math. He wants **math games**. Fine. That's the whole strategy.

I'm good at math. Always was. It's the subject I never had to fake interest in. Math going to a franchise center would have felt like **handing off the one thing I'm actually built to teach.**

Handwriting is different. I'm not outsourcing the whole thing - Kumon gets the daily pencil habit he won't do for me. **Home gets the handwriting I think Kumon can't do** - his ideas, his quiz cards, his sentences about stuff he cares about. Kumon copies lines. I want him to handwrite words that *mean* something.

There's a selfish part and an honest part. Selfish: I want to be the parent he associates with numbers. Honest: he's six, math feels hard next to reading, and **nobody learns to love a subject that always feels like losing.** Games first. One printed line later, if he earned it. Passion from me comes through the game - the pattern, the cheat code, the "you got me" moment - not a lecture.

## Why math stayed with me

I did a Kumon math trial once, years ago. My answers were correct. The feedback wasn't "you don't understand this." It was **you could do it faster.**

That stuck. Kumon wasn't trying to make me love numbers. It was trying to shave seconds off a row of problems I'd already solved. Speed as the next rung on the ladder. For handwriting copy work with a six-year-old who avoids the pencil, fine - you need reps. For math with a kid who might actually enjoy the subject? I didn't want **faster** to become the whole point.

Kumon math would have been efficient. Someone else's ladder, someone else's red pen, someone else watching the clock.

I didn't want efficient. I wanted **mine**.

Not because Kumon instructors are bad. Because math is where I'm strongest and he's still six - but **hard is his word for math right now**, and I get it. Reading is easy wins. Numbers take a step he doesn't always see yet. This is the window where a parent who likes the subject can sneak it in as a game. Kumon can keep the copy-work reps. **Home is where math stops feeling like the hard subject.**

Same logic for handwriting at home. Kumon can make his letters neater through repetition. Home has to make legible handwriting worth the effort - words he picked, someone who reads them, stop before the pencil goes limp.

## What Kumon reading is for (and what it isn't)

His reading track nails a few things I'm glad to rent:

- **Show up most days.** Small reps beat a Saturday cram.
- **Easy stuff first.** Confidence before challenge.
- **Someone else enforces the pencil** when he'd rather disappear.

He already reads well. Kumon reading is **handwriting under another name** - tracing, copying, spelling rows. That's useful. It's also the same factory I walked through on my math trial: correct, then faster, then more.

Kumon won't teach him to **want** to pick up the pencil. It will teach him to complete handwriting drills. Neater letters. Fewer complaints at the center. Fine.

What Kumon reading won't give him:

- A sentence about **his** day, not a workbook's day.
- Words from a **quiz card he picked**, not a line chosen for the whole class.
- Handwriting tied to something on the tablet or the sheet - a game he won, a drawing, a postcard to lola.
- Stopping while it still feels fun, not when the packet ends.

That's the home handwriting program. Kumon is the gym. Home is the sport.

## Why he said no to math (and what he actually needs)

I didn't argue when he chose reading. We wanted the handwriting reps anyway. Kumon math on top would have been **hard math with a timer** - the worst combo for a kid who already knows which subject costs him more effort.

His reason wasn't "I'm bad at math." He's six. He counts fine when it's a game on the screen. He keeps score on the tablet when he's winning. **Worksheet math feels harder than reading** - more steps, more wrong guesses, more sitting still while his sister's page looks easier. The Kumon *format* would have piled on top of that. He didn't reject numbers. He rejected **hard rows**.

What he asked for instead: **games.** Not "fun math worksheets." Games. Shuffle. Score. Beat dad. Close the tab when you're done.

So the home program has two jobs Kumon won't do well: **math games that sneak in the hard part later**, and **handwriting he'll own.** Tablet first. One printed line only if he still has gas. Stop before the glazed look.

If home feels like Kumon with a different logo - or like school homework - I've already lost.

## The principles (and how they beat Kumon reading)

Kumon reading is built for **volume, speed, and sameness** - same pages for every kid, neater and faster until the next level. I learned on my own trial that "correct" isn't enough there. You have to be **fast**.

Home is built on different rules. Ten of them. **Five, six, and seven are the handwriting half** - the part I actually want better than Kumon rows. The week below follows all ten.

| Principle | What Kumon reading does | What home does instead |
|-----------|-------------------------|------------------------|
| **1. Want-to-come-back** (intrinsic motivation) | Finish the packet. Next level waits. | Stop while he still has gas. Tired face = done. "Try again." Laugh. Move on. |
| **2. Two layers, not double homework** (skill practice vs. transfer) | Kumon owns the pencil reps. | Kumon = gym. Home = sport. No copy pages after center. |
| **3. Games, not factories** (game-based learning) | Quiet table, timed rows. | **Math games on the tablet** - shuffle, score, beat dad - then maybe one printed line. |
| **4. Easy wins before hard rows** (scaffolding) | Next level whether you're ready or not. | Start where he wins. Reading is easy - math stays on easy until he asks for more. |
| **5. His words on paper** (authentic literacy) | Same worksheet for every kid. Copy row seventeen. | He picks the words. He has to **care** what it says - a quiz card, his drawing, his postcard - or we switch sheets. |
| **6. Say it, then handwrite it** (language experience approach) | Pencil from minute one. Trace and copy cold. | Mouth wins first. He talks. I write once. He copies. Spelling and letter shape fixed together. |
| **7. Real audience** (authentic audience) | Sheet goes in a folder. | Note on the fridge, postcard to lola, sign on his door - **someone reads what he wrote.** |
| **8. Passion transfers** (modeling) | Instructor enforces pace. | I get excited when he sees the pattern. He can tell. |
| **9. Concrete before abstract** (CPA: concrete-pictorial-abstract) | Symbols on a page. | Picture cards on the tablet first - then one equation on the math line. |
| **10. Explain it back** (metacognition) | Neater letters, faster completion. | "Tell me how you got that." Not "fill the row." |

**The short version:** Kumon reading asks *did you finish, and was it fast enough?* Home asks *did you want to play again?* For math - **hard** is his word, games are his word. For handwriting - *did he pick up the pencil without a fight, and can someone else read it?*

Principles **3**, **4**, and **9** lean math. **5**, **6**, and **7** lean handwriting. Principle **4** is the only math-only gate: never open with the hard part. Open with the game. The equation on paper is the trophy, not the assignment.

## The setup: tablet plus printed sheets

Two tools. That's the whole kit. **No counting toys, no bowls of snacks, no extra props** - tablet and printed sheets only.

**Tablet** for **math games** - anything oral, fast, winnable. **Printed sheets** for handwriting and the one math line he earns after the game.

Kumon is already pencil-heavy at the center. Home doesn't need a third stack of worksheets - especially not for a kid who already thinks math is the **hard** subject. The tablet is where math stops looking like homework. The printer is where he keeps something real - a note, a label, **one** equation he chose to handwrite because he won the round.

### What the tablet is for (two quiz decks)

Two decks on the same [randomizer](/randomizer/). Same game: shuffle, guess out loud, reveal, next.

- **[Grade 1 quiz](/randomizer/?set=grade-1-quiz)** - animals, colors, facts. **No arithmetic.** We call five cards "beat dad." Mon-Thu lives here.
- **[Grade 1 math quiz](/randomizer/?set=grade-1-math-quiz)** - addition, subtraction, bonds. Separate deck so math never ambushes a warmup round. One card from here when he's ready - then maybe the math line sheet.
- **"I'm thinking of a number"** - bigger than 4, smaller than 8. Oral on the tablet, no second deck needed.
- **Beat your score** - how many cards in two minutes? Only count the ones he got without help.
- **Quick rounds** - close the tab when he says done. Principle **1**. Hard subject needs an easy exit.
- **Offline copy** - save either quiz HTML for spotty wifi. Same games, no loading spinner.

What the tablet is **not** for: long drills, timed rows, "you're not fast enough." That's Kumon. That's also what **hard** feels like to him.

What the tablet is also **not** for: copy work, tracing, journals. Handwriting stays on paper.

### What we print

I keep four half-page templates on rotation. Print a week's batch Sunday night - usually 6 sheets, not 30.

| Sheet | What's on it | When we use it |
|-------|----------------|----------------|
| **Math line** | Name, date, one blank equation `___ + ___ = ___`, one "tell me how" line | After a tablet round - one written sum only |
| **Journal** | "Today I ___." plus two wide lines | He says it, I write a model on the side, he copies |
| **Draw + label** | Empty box, three label lines under it | His picture, his words |
| **Message** | Postcard or note layout - To / From, four lines | lola, mom, door sign he tapes up |

All black and white. One sheet per day max for handwriting. Math line can share the back of yesterday's scrap if he only used the front.

**Why print at all?** Principle **7** - real audience. A note on the fridge beats a file. Kumon's sheets go in a folder at the center. His postcard actually gets mailed.

**Why not print everything?** A thick home workbook becomes Kumon with a different logo. Tablet handles the part Kumon over-papers: drill without the packet face.

## The daily shape: tablet, then one printed sheet

Same time most days - after snack, before free play. **Ten to fifteen minutes total.** Principles **1** and **2** govern everything.

Typical flow:

1. **Math game on the tablet** (5-8 min) - quiz shuffle, guess the number, beat dad. He has to **want** to open it.
2. **Printed sheet** (5-7 min) - handwriting always. Math line **only if the game went well** and he still has gas.

Bad math day? Game only. Skip the equation. Principle **4** beats principle **6** when hard is already in his head.

### Math: game first, one printed line is the trophy

Math feels **hard**. The tablet is where we hide the work inside the game (principles **3** and **4**):

- **Easy cards first** - general quiz on the tablet. Math deck only when he still has gas.
- **Pictures on screen, numbers on paper** - quiz cards do the concrete part; the math line sheet holds one equation.
- **Out loud on the tablet** - he talks, I react. Wrong guess is "nice try," not a red mark.
- **One math line sheet** - only after a round he enjoyed. `3 + 2 = 5` once. Optional on rough days.

No printed page of twenty problems. The pencil line is proof he **won** the game, not punishment for picking the hard subject.

### Handwriting: always printed, never Kumon-style copy pages

Home handwriting follows principles **5**, **6**, and **7** on paper he can give someone:

- **Journal sheet** - he says it, I write a model in small print, he copies on the lines.
- **Draw + label sheet** - his picture, his three words.
- **Message sheet** - postcard to lola, thank-you note, door sign he tapes up.

If he can't tell me why we're putting it on paper, we pick a different sheet. Kumon's pages are identical for every kid. We print a blank journal line and he handwrites **his** sentence - one he already said out loud, not a workbook paragraph.

## The quiz decks on the tablet (his favorite math game so far)

Worksheets alone will lose him. He said so. **Hard.**

Same flow for both decks:

1. Tap shuffle.
2. He guesses out loud.
3. Tap reveal - did you beat dad?
4. Again. Five cards. Done.

**[Grade 1 quiz](/randomizer/?set=grade-1-quiz)** is the warmup - animals, colors, no plus or minus. He asks for this one by name because it's a **game**, not a test.

**[Grade 1 math quiz](/randomizer/?set=grade-1-math-quiz)** is the separate math deck - addition, subtraction, bonds. Principle **4**: easy cards first, always. We pull **one** math card into a round only when the general cards went well. Wrong guess is still "nice try."

That's principles **3**, **4**, and **6** on glass. Kumon wants a pencil from minute one on the **hard** subject. The tablet lets him win with his mouth first. **Then** - if he wants - the math line sheet gets one equation as a trophy.

## The week

One week. Same shape every day: **tablet first, one printed sheet second.** Ten to fifteen minutes. Sunday off.

**Principles in play:** 4, 3, 6, 5 - plus **7** on Saturday (postcard).  
**Math:** general quiz Mon-Thu; **one** card from the [math quiz](/randomizer/?set=grade-1-math-quiz) on Fri if he still has gas. Math line optional all week.  
**Handwriting:** labels, journal, copy one sentence he picks, one message someone will read.

| Day | Tablet (math game) | Printed sheet |
|-----|-------------------|---------------|
| Mon | [General quiz](/randomizer/?set=grade-1-quiz) - beat dad, 5 easy cards | Math line optional: one digit from a card he won |
| Tue | [General quiz](/randomizer/?set=grade-1-quiz) - guess the number 1-10 | Draw + label: house `door` `roof` `me` |
| Wed | [General quiz](/randomizer/?set=grade-1-quiz) - he picks animals | Journal: *Today I beat dad.* |
| Thu | [General quiz](/randomizer/?set=grade-1-quiz) - 5 easy cards | Copy one sentence he picks from a card or journal |
| Fri | [Math quiz](/randomizer/?set=grade-1-math-quiz) - 1 easy card, then 4 general if he wants | Journal: `mom` `park` `fun` in one sentence |
| Sat | He picks the deck - general or math | Message sheet: postcard to lola |
| Sun | Off | Off |

Batch-print six sheets Saturday night if you're the plan-ahead type. I am not, usually - but four half-page templates make it a five-minute job. Stack them by the tablet charger.

**Math games that don't feel hard** (tablet only):

- "Beat dad" on the [general quiz](/randomizer/?set=grade-1-quiz) - five cards, low stakes.
- "I'm thinking of a number" - he narrows: *bigger? smaller?*
- One card from the [math quiz](/randomizer/?set=grade-1-math-quiz) - only after a general round went well.
- "One more card" - he taps shuffle again before you say done. No paper yet.
- Dad gets one wrong on purpose. He catches you. He laughs. Math isn't only losing.

**Handwriting shape most days:** he **says** it first on the tablet or out loud (principle **6**). Then copy, label, or journal on a printed sheet. Thursday is copy-from-card - transcription reps on words he already won in the game (principle **5**).

### After the week

If he asks to shuffle again, or grabs a blank sheet without being told, add a second math card next week. If not, **run the same week again** (principle **1**). Kumon would advance the packet. I'd rather he wants to open the tablet and pick up the pencil.

## What I'm not trying to do

- **Replace Kumon reading.** That's the rep layer - pencil habit, copy rows, someone else enforcing it. Home math and home handwriting are the **better** layer on top.
- **Duplicate Kumon at home.** No printed workbook. No thirty-page packs. No extra props. Four half-page templates, one face per day, plus the tablet.
- **Make a prodigy.** I want him comfortable with numbers, curious about puzzles, and handwritten notes someone else can read - not a six-year-old who dreads pencils.
- **Win a comparison.** Some kid in Kumon math will blast past us on paper. Fine. I'm playing a longer game.
- **Force enthusiasm.** Week one win = he asks for another round. Not "loves math." Just doesn't dread it.

---

## How I'll know it's working

Not test scores. Wrong metric at six. He still says math is **hard**. I'm watching for whether it stays hard-but-playable, or hard-and-he-runs.

**He asks to play the quiz.** **He catches dad's wrong answer on purpose.** **He says "five more cards" before I say done.**

For handwriting: **he picks the sentence to copy** on the printed sheet. **He reads his own note out loud** and tapes it somewhere real. **His Kumon sheets get neater** - that's Kumon's scoreboard.

The big win: he doesn't groan when I open the tablet to math games. Someday maybe he brings a puzzle from school. Week one? **He stopped treating math like the subject you only do when you're trapped.**

If he starts hiding when I say "ten minutes," we only do handwriting that day - or skip entirely (principle **1**). **Never fight the hard subject head-on.** Close the tablet. Try a game tomorrow.

## If your kid chose one Kumon and not the other

That's useful data. Don't treat the "no" subject like a problem to fix with more of what they rejected.

- **Honor the yes.** We picked Kumon reading for rep work - pencil habit, copy drills. Let it do that job.
- **Build the better layer at home.** If math feels hard, lead with games - not harder worksheets. Kumon won't make him own his handwriting either. Different format, his words, stop early.
- **Start tiny.** One "beat dad" round on the tablet. Skip the math line if he's done. Add a sheet when he asks.

He's six weeks into Kumon reading and we're early on the tablet-and-printer routine. He still says math is **hard**. He also asks for the quiz sometimes. That's enough for now. Kumon for reps at the center. **Math games on the tablet, handwriting on paper he keeps** - sneak the hard part in after the win.
