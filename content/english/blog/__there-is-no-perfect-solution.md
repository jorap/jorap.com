I used to stall decisions by researching one more option. One more theme. One more hosting plan. One more framework thread at 1 AM.

The hidden belief: **somewhere there's a perfect choice** with no downsides. If I just look harder, I'll find it.

That's the **nirvana fallacy** - comparing real options to an imaginary ideal, then rejecting everything real because it isn't flawless.

---

## Perfect solutions are rare. Tradeoffs are guaranteed.

A tradeoff means **giving up something to get something else.**

- Fast and cheap rarely pairs with fully custom
- Simple tools sacrifice flexibility
- Flexible tools sacrifice simplicity
- Own your platform → you maintain it
- Rent a platform → they change the rules

Every real decision sits on that spectrum. The mistake is pretending one option has no cost.

---

## How the fallacy shows up in my work

**Web development:** "I'll wait for the perfect theme / stack / CMS." Meanwhile the site doesn't exist.

**Note-taking:** "I'll migrate when I find the perfect Second Brain app." Meanwhile ideas die in random text files.

**Gear:** "I'll buy when the perfect router / phone / printer exists." Meanwhile the broken one wastes an hour a week.

**Content:** "I'll publish when the post is perfect." Meanwhile the draft ages out of relevance.

In each case, the **cost of waiting** never gets a line item. It should.

---

## What I do instead

**Name the tradeoff out loud.** "I'm choosing Hugo + Cloudflare because it's free and fast, and I'm accepting that I'll edit Markdown in a repo instead of a clicky admin."

**Set a decision deadline.** Research until Friday. Pick. Ship. Revisit in three months with data, not anxiety.

**Ask 'compared to what?'** The alternative isn't fantasy-perfect. It's usually **nothing**, or the broken status quo.

**Good enough for the job** beats theoretically optimal for a job I don't have yet.

---

## When "perfect" is worth waiting

Not everything should be rushed:

- Irreversible commitments (some contracts, some migrations)
- Safety-critical choices
- Foundations you'll live in for years **if** you've already validated the need

Even then, it's **informed tradeoffs**, not imaginary perfection.

---

## Bottom line

There is often **no perfect solution** - only options with costs you'll live with.

Choose the tradeoff you can sustain, ship it, and fix forward. The nirvana fallacy feels like wisdom. It's usually procrastination in a thoughtful hat.
