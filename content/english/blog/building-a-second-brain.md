How many good ideas have you had and forgotten? How many articles saved to "read later" never resurfaced when you actually needed them?

I built [ideas.jorap.com](https://ideas.jorap.com/) because my head isn't a reliable filing cabinet. **Building a Second Brain** — Tiago Forte's framing for an external, trusted system for what you learn — is the philosophy behind it.

Not a productivity performance. A survival strategy for anyone who consumes more than they can remember.

---

## Brains are for having ideas, not storing them

We read, listen, watch, and bookmark at a pace our memory can't match. The pressure to keep learning is real. The follow-through — actually using what we learned — is where most systems collapse.

A Second Brain is an **external, centralized, digital repository** for ideas, resources, and connections. It expands what you can recall and reuse without pretending you'll remember that perfect quote from a podcast three Tuesdays ago.

---

## The CODE method

Fort's framework is **CODE**: Capture, Organize, Distill, Express. I use it as a checklist, not a religion.

### Capture

Save what resonates — quotes, links, half-formed thoughts, screenshots, recipes, code snippets. The bar isn't "might be useful someday." It's **"this changes how I think or what I'd do."**

Questions I ask before capturing:

- What themes keep showing up in my work and life?
- What high-value information do I already have access to?
- What do I want to mix, match, and resurface later?

Tools don't matter as much as **one trusted inbox**. TiddlyWiki, Obsidian, Notion, plain Markdown in a repo — pick something you'll actually open.

### Organize

Don't build a perfect folder tree on day one. **Organize for action** — by project, area, or whatever you'll need when you sit down to work.

If a note doesn't connect to something you're building, maintaining, or learning, it's archive bait.

### Distill

Long notes are graveyards. Summarize in layers: headline, bullets, pull quotes. **Design notes for future-you** who's tired and in a hurry.

Progressive summarization beats rewriting everything. Bold the good sentence today. Add a summary paragraph next month when you use it.

### Express

The point isn't hoarding. It's **shipping** — blog posts, recipes, client work, sermons, decks, code.

Your Second Brain should make the blank page less blank. You're not starting from zero; you're assembling from parts you've already collected.

## PARA: a simple organization lens

Another Forte lens I use alongside CODE:

- **Projects** — things with a deadline
- **Areas** — ongoing responsibilities (health, finances, home)
- **Resources** — topics of interest
- **Archives** — inactive stuff, out of the way but searchable

Not every note needs a perfect PARA home immediately. "Is this active?" is enough to start.

## Why TiddlyWiki for my Second Brain

I wanted something **portable, ownable, and publishable**. TiddlyWiki is a single HTML file (or a small static site) that works offline, syncs through Git, and deploys free on Cloudflare Pages.

That's the stack behind ideas.jorap.com: **TiddlyWiki + GitHub + Cloudflare Pages** — the same sustainable publishing idea I wrote about for JoRap's World, applied to a private-public idea garden.

Your stack might differ. The principle doesn't: **own the notes, own the export, own the publish path.**

---

## Common mistakes

- **Capturing everything** — becomes noise; be selective
- **Over-organizing before creating** — folders feel productive; they're often procrastination
- **Never expressing** — a Second Brain that only grows is a hoard
- **Tool-hopping** — migration feels like work; shipping feels like work; pick one

---

## Start smaller than you think

One inbox. One weekly review. One note distilled enough to reuse. One thing published from what you captured.

The system grows when it proves it saved you time — not when the setup looks impressive.

---

## Bottom line

A Second Brain isn't about remembering everything. It's about **not losing the ideas that matter** and having them ready when it's time to build, cook, write, or decide.

That's the job ideas.jorap.com was hired to do. CODE and PARA are just how I try to do it without drowning in my own tabs.
