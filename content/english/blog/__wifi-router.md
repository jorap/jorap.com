I used to treat wifi routers like printer ink - buy whatever's on sale, plug it in, forget about it until something breaks. Then the video call drops during a meeting, the kid's homework tab spins forever, and suddenly the cheapest router on the shelf doesn't feel like such a smart save.

These days I have a short checklist before I buy or recommend one. Not a spec sheet. A "will this actually work in my house" list.

---

## Coverage beats top speed on the box

Manufacturers love printing big numbers on the packaging. **AC1900! AX6000!** Most of that is theoretical lab math you'll never see while making adobo and streaming at the same time.

What I care about is whether the signal reaches the rooms I actually use - bedroom, kitchen, home office, wherever the router ends up shoved behind a shelf. If I have dead zones, I'd rather add a mesh node or move the router than chase the highest Mbps number on the spec sheet.

Before I buy, I sketch the floor plan and mark where people sit with laptops and phones. That one step saves more grief than reading router reviews for an hour.

---

## Stability over flash features

Guest networks, parental controls, VPN servers, USB ports - routers love bundling features most homes never touch. What I want first is **boring reliability**: stays up through a rainstorm of devices, doesn't need a weekly reboot, doesn't randomly forget that the printer exists.

A router that "mostly works" is worse than one that's merely adequate but never surprises you. Mystery reboots at 2 AM are how routers end up in the trash.

---

## Placement is half the battle

The best router in the world loses if it's buried in a closet, sitting on the floor behind a metal cabinet, or tucked under the TV stand with seventeen HDMI cables around it.

I try to put it **central and elevated** - hallway shelf, top of a bookcase, somewhere the signal can spread without fighting walls and appliances. If I can't do that, I stop pretending a single router will cover three floors and plan for mesh or an access point instead.

---

## Easy firmware updates (and a plan when support ends)

Security patches matter. I want a router that makes updates **obvious and painless**, not something I have to hunt for on a forum every six months.

I also check how long the manufacturer actually supports the model. A cheap router with no more firmware updates is a ticking box on your network. I'd rather pay a little more for something that'll get patches for a few years.

---

## Wired ports still matter

Everything is wireless until it isn't. The desktop, the TV box, the printer that refuses to join wifi gracefully - I want **at least a few gigabit LAN ports** on the back.

If the router is also my modem combo unit, I pay extra attention to heat and ventilation. Combo boxes work until they don't, and when they fail you lose two things at once.

---

## When mesh makes sense

A single router works fine in a small apartment. The moment you have **multiple floors, thick walls, or a long narrow house**, mesh stops being luxury and starts being math.

I'm not loyal to one mesh brand - I just want nodes that are easy to add and don't need an app account I'll forget the password to. Simple setup, clear indicator lights, done.

---

## The shortlist, again

If I'm shopping for a home wifi router today, I scan for:

1. Coverage for the rooms we actually use - not lab Mbps
2. Stability; no ritual reboots
3. Sensible placement, or a mesh plan if placement can't be fixed
4. Firmware support that isn't already end-of-life
5. Enough wired ports for the stubborn devices
6. Setup my family won't need me to redo every month

---

## What I'd do right now

If my router died tomorrow, I'd map the house first, check what's still getting security updates, and buy for **coverage and calm** - not the biggest number on the box. The internet coming into the house is only as good as the wifi that actually reaches your chair.
