People picture a Second Brain as a beautiful note app. Mine is that - but it's also **how I use the browser**, what I subscribe to on purpose, and the small shortcuts that stop me from drowning in tabs.

These are the daily habits from my presentation notes, the ones that survived contact with real life.

---

## Browser: middle click is underrated

**Middle-click a link** - open in a new tab without leaving the page. Middle-click a tab - close it. Sounds trivial until you research something with twenty sources and your hand never leaves the mouse.

**Middle-click a bookmarks folder** - open the whole folder at once. Great for morning dashboards: weather, calendar, news, tools.

I wrote a full post on the mouse wheel click if you want the longer version. Short version: **it's the best browser shortcut nobody talks about.**

---

## Chrome sync for shortcuts

Bookmarks and shortcuts should **follow you across machines**. Work laptop, home desktop, travel - same baseline setup.

I keep folders boring and named by job:

- Daily work
- Daily personal
- Reference (docs I always forget the URL for)

If I type it twice in a week, it becomes a bookmark.

---

## Curated feeds beat the default timeline

Social apps optimize for engagement, not value. I **narrow the firehose** on purpose.

**Facebook - Favorites feed:** `facebook.com/?filter=favorites&sk=h_chr` - people and pages I actually chose, not whatever the algorithm is pushing today. I wrote more about this in my Favorites Feed post; it's still one of the best hidden upgrades on the platform.

**YouTube - Subscriptions feed:** `youtube.com/feed/subscriptions` - channels I subscribed to on purpose. Not the homepage. Not Shorts roulette. The list I asked for.

The pattern: **pick the narrow view, bookmark it, make it the default entry point.**

---

## 20% time savings: 1.25× playback

For podcasts, lectures, and long explainers, **1.25× speed** is the sweet spot - faster without chipmunk chaos. You adapt in a day.

For dense technical content I stay at 1×. For recap and opinion, 1.25× or 1.5× saves real calendar time over a month.

---

## AI in the loop (not the whole loop)

My capture stack for video and long text:

1. **YouTube transcript** - get the words without rewatching
2. **Prompt splitter** - break huge transcripts into chunks the model can handle
3. **Structured prompt** - summarize, extract actions, format for notes

AI doesn't replace the Second Brain. It **accelerates capture and distill** so more stuff worth keeping actually lands in the wiki.

Tools change names monthly. The workflow doesn't: **transcript → chunk → summarize → save to TiddlyWiki.**

---

## CODE and PARA, daily edition

Every day touches all four:

| Step | Daily example |
|---|---|
| **Capture** | Save link, clip quote, voice memo |
| **Organize** | Tag by project or area |
| **Distill** | Three-bullet summary at top of tiddler |
| **Express** | Blog draft, client note, recipe test |

**PARA** keeps active projects visible. Everything else is resource or archive. Morning glance: what project tiddlers moved yesterday?

---

## Bottom line

A Second Brain daily workflow isn't mystical. It's **better defaults**: the right feed, the right browser habits, slightly faster media, and a capture path short enough that you'll actually use it.

Notes are the archive. Habits are the pump.
