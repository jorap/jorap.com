I've installed enough browsers to fill a short blog post on its own. Chrome because everything "just works." Firefox because I wanted to feel noble about privacy. Edge because it shipped on a Windows laptop and I was too tired to uninstall it on day one.

**Brave** is the one that stuck for personal browsing. Not because it's perfect. Because it does the boring job I actually care about - **keep the junk off my screen without turning browser setup into a hobby** - and it's still Chromium enough that the web doesn't fight me.

Chrome stays on my machine too. It just doesn't get my personal tab forest.

---

## I got tired of negotiating with ads

Most of my reading happens in a browser: docs, forum threads, a recipe I'll forget by dinner, three articles open because I never learned to close tabs.

On a stock Chromium install, that rhythm turns into a negotiation. Accept cookies. Dismiss the newsletter popup. Wait for the video ad to finish loading before the page scrolls smoothly. Install an ad blocker. Update the ad blocker. Wonder why the ad blocker broke checkout on one random Shopee seller.

Brave ships with **Shields already on**. Trackers and a lot of the noisy stuff get blocked before I think about it. I'm not pretending that makes me invisible on the internet. It does mean fewer "your computer has a virus" banners and less RAM burned on autoplay junk I'll never watch.

The first week felt almost suspicious - pages loading faster, fewer modals, fewer tabs I opened and immediately regretted. Then I stopped noticing, which is the point. **A tool that works should disappear into the background.**

---

## Chromium underneath, so the web still behaves

I've bounced off browsers that were philosophically pure and practically annoying. Sites that assumed Chrome. Logins that broke in weird ways. Extensions I relied on that didn't exist or hadn't been updated since 2019.

Brave is **Chromium with a different coat of paint**. Hugo builds, Google Docs, WordPress admin, Bitwarden, the dark-mode extension I refuse to give up - they run the way I've learned to expect. I'm not retraining my hands for a different engine just to make a point about the open web.

That matters on freelance days when a client sends a staging link at 10 PM and I need it to open without a troubleshooting detour. Personal Brave and work Chrome share the same rendering guts. The split is about **what I want each profile to remember**, not whether the page will load.

---

## Brave for me, Chrome for work

This is the part people argue about, so I'll say it plainly.

**Brave** gets reading, research, shopping, YouTube rabbit holes, the tabs that aren't billable. That's where I want shields up and Google seeing less of my off-hours wandering.

**Chrome** gets its own profile for client work: WordPress logins, dev tools, the extension stack I need on builds, Google Meet when a call lands in the browser instead of the app. I also **test every site through Chrome first** because it's still what most visitors use. Brave can look fine while Chrome shows the broken layout. I've shipped a fix based on Brave-only testing exactly once. Never again.

Two browsers sounds like overhead. For me it's **less mental overhead** - I know which window holds client credentials and which one holds the weird forum thread about extension cords. Close Chrome at the end of a work block and the day stops following me into the couch scroll.

---

## Brave Sync is the reason phone and desktop feel like one browser

I save links at the wrong time constantly. A forum thread on the couch. A product page someone sent in Messenger while I'm in line. A Hugo doc I meant to read at the desk. If that bookmark only lives on the phone, it's gone by morning - buried under three other tabs and whatever notification cleared my head.

**Brave Sync** is what keeps personal browsing from splitting into two amnesiac halves.

### No Google account required

Chrome sync wants a Google login. That's fine for work - my client Chrome profile already lives inside that world. I didn't want **personal** reading and shopping tabs tied to the same account that sees my Gmail and my client Drive folders.

Brave Sync uses a **sync chain** instead. You set it up once on the desktop, get a QR code or a recovery phrase, scan or paste it on the phone, and the two installs start talking. No new cloud account. No "sign in with Google so we can remember your tabs." Just encrypted sync between devices you paired on purpose.

That matches how I use the split: **Google identity for billable work, Brave sync chain for everything else.**

### What I actually sync

I turn on the boring stuff that saves me from texting links to myself:

- **Bookmarks** - the main win. Save on phone, open at the desk.
- **History** - when I know I read something this week but can't remember which tab forest it lived in.
- **Settings and shields** - so I don't re-tune the same site on every device.

I **don't** sync passwords through Brave. Bitwarden already does that everywhere, work and personal, phone and laptop. One vault, one job. I'm not maintaining a second password store because the browser offered.

Extensions are hit or miss. Some sync their settings. Some don't. I stopped expecting the whole extension garden to clone perfectly and treated sync as **bookmarks + habits**, not a full disk image of my desktop install.

### Setup is a one-time afternoon, not a lifestyle

The first pairing is slightly fiddly. Open sync on the desktop, generate the chain, scan the QR on the phone, wait, confirm it connected. Write down the recovery phrase somewhere sane - password manager note, paper in the desk drawer, whatever you'd actually use when a phone dies.

I did that once per device family and moved on. It's not as invisible as Chrome sync after you've been logged into Google for a decade. It's also **not renting your bookmark list to an ad company** just because sync was convenient.

### When it hiccups

Brave Sync is not magic. Sometimes a bookmark lands on the phone ten minutes late. Sometimes I open the desktop and the bar looks stale until I flip sync off and on like a router reboot joke.

The worst failure mode is losing the chain. New phone, wiped install, kid long-presses the wrong button - if you don't have that recovery phrase, you start over. I learned that the paranoid way after assuming "it's in the cloud somewhere" like every other sync story. **Brave's cloud can't recover what you didn't save.** That's the point, and also the responsibility.

Most of what I save still ends up in notes or repo files if it matters long-term. Sync is for **the handoff between devices this week**, not my permanent archive. When sync glitches, I notice less than if Bitwarden broke. When sync works, I stop emailing myself URLs like it's 2012.

## What I tried and put back on the shelf

**Firefox** is a serious browser. I used it for stretches, liked the mission, and still hit enough "works in Chrome" friction on client-adjacent stuff that I drifted back. Maybe that's on the sites, not Mozilla. I still don't want to fight the web during a deadline.

**Safari** on a borrowed Mac felt great for reading until I needed the same extension lineup I run on WordPress builds. Apple's browser story is basically "stay on Apple hardware." I don't.

**Edge** is fine. It's also the browser that exists because Windows needed a default tab. I never developed opinions about it beyond "sure, that opens links."

Brave won because it sat in the **least annoying middle**: Chromium compatibility, aggressive blocking by default, no sermon about switching my whole life to a new ecosystem.

## Brave Origin on my Linux desktop

My personal browsing on the **Linux desktop** runs **Brave Origin**. Not because I read a manifesto about minimalist browsers. Because **Origin is free on Linux**.

Brave sells Origin as a one-time purchase on Windows and macOS. On Linux you download it or upgrade from regular Brave in Settings, click "Proceed with Origin for free on Linux," and you're done. No license fee. No account circus. Same shields, same Chromium underneath, same sync chain to my phone.

That's the whole pitch for me. I'm not paying for a browser variant when the free-on-my-OS version exists. Linux gets treated like a first-class citizen here, which is rare enough that I noticed.

Origin also happens to be the stripped-down build - wallet, rewards, AI sidebar clutter, most of the upsell noise turned off by default. I would've ignored that stuff in regular Brave anyway. **Free on Linux plus less junk is a nice two-for-one**, not the reason I switched.

Regular Brave stays on Android. Origin stays on the desktop. Sync still ties them together.

---

## On phone and desktop

**Desktop (Linux):** Brave Origin for personal links - free on Linux, which is why it's there. Chrome sits one click away for client QA.

**Phone:** Regular Brave on Android - shields on, Chromium-ish behavior, less garbage in mobile article views. Banking and OTP-heavy stuff still goes through the slow second phone I wrote about elsewhere. Habit, not Brave doctrine.

The two installs stay aligned through **Brave Sync**, not because I manually copy links between devices. Same personal browser, two screens.

---

## What I give up

Brave is still a Chromium fork backed by a company with its own incentives. Shields don't make me anonymous. Some sites break until I lower shields for that domain, and then I forget and wonder why checkout acts weird three months later.

Google still sees plenty when I'm in Chrome for work. That's the trade I accepted: **split identities, not pure privacy theater.**

RAM usage is still browser RAM usage. Two Chromium profiles don't magically use less memory because one of them has a lion icon.

Lose the sync chain without saving the recovery phrase and you're rebuilding bookmarks by hand. I keep the phrase in Bitwarden for exactly that reason.

And if Brave-the-company does something I hate tomorrow, I can move. Bookmarks export. Bitwarden doesn't care which icon I clicked. I'm not married to the brand. I'm married to the workflow.

---

## What I actually run

**Personal browsing:** Brave Origin on the Linux desktop (free there), regular Brave on Android, sync chain paired once, shields on, Bitwarden for passwords, dark mode.

**Client work:** Chrome in its own profile, dev tools open, test here first.

**Everything else:** Terminal, Hugo, the apps that need a real computer - same laptop post I already wrote about.

That's the setup: **Brave for my tabs, Chrome for everyone else's reality.** Not the most ideological answer. Just the one that stopped me from fighting the web on my own time.
